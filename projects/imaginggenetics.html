Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) is a chronic metabolic disease that gradually develops through progressive impairment of glucose metabolism. It is induced through a combination of environmental and genetic risk factors. Elevated fasting glucose levels, hypertension, obesity and/or dyslipidemia — also known as the 'metabolic syndrome (MetS)' — are commonly observed environmental risk factors in subjects at risk to develop T2DM. However, the complex genetic nature remains largely unknown. Eventually, T2DM may lead to damage of many organs, including the cardiovascular and/or central nervous systems. In this project, I focus on the genetical and neuroradiological differences in subjects affected by T2DM (N=44) and MetS (N=44), compared to controls (N=45), using whole-exome sequencing and 7T MRI, respectively.

— Hippocampal correlates of memory impairment in T2DM

Memory problems are frequently observed with T2DM, however, the structural and functional brain changes, measured using MRI, remain poorly understood, specifically with respect to the hippocampal formation (HF), a complex brain structure that plays an important role in memory functioning. Insufficient spatial resolution in typical clinical scans and a lack of tailored analysis methods have hampered progress in elucidating these hippocampal changes at the substructural level. In this project, we will use the 7T MRI dataset, behavioral data, and a novel analysis method to study changes in the HF associated with T2DM at a finer scale than previously possible. Given the high prevalence of T2DM, the results will provide important and refined insights in the neural markers of memory impairment in T2DM, and a novel framework for similar future investigations in other neurodegenerative diseases.
