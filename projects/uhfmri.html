Development of ultra-high field (UHF) strength (7 Tesla) MRI scanners increases the possibilities, compared to conventional MRI scanners, to obtain structural and blood oxygenation level-dependent (BOLD) functional MRI (fMRI) data with an unprecedented spatial resolution, without compromising data quality or increasing scanning time. In addition, novel post-processing strategies that enable analyses of complex and convoluted structures become more and more available.

Hippocampal formation (with dr. Ali R. Khan and prof. Stefan Kohler)

The hippocampal formation (HF) was usually — due to its small scale — characterized as a single homogeneous structure using MRI, and treated as so. However, due to the developments in neuroimaging techniques and analyses methods nowadays enable detection of fine-scale features of the hippocampal formation. As a result, morphological (e.g. cortical thickness), microstructural (e.g. myelination level), as well as the functional properties (e.g. functional connectivity) of the different hippocampal subfields — the subiculum, CA1 to 4 and dentate gyrus — can subsequently be mapped into this unfolded space. By combining these two methodological developments, it is now possible to study the hippocampal formation in greater detail under varying conditions (e.g. healthy vs. diseased).

Recent studies have evaluated some of the morphological, microstructural and functional characteristics within and across subfields, but the origins of the observed differences remain elusive. Based on regional differences in vascular density and vulnerabilty for vascular patohlogies, it has been hypothesized that the degree of vascularization of the hippocampus plays a profound role in the differentiation of the hippocampal subfields. However, this has not been studied in vivo yet. Here, I propose to examine the role of the hippocampal formation's vascularization in the differentiation of its subfields, both at the structural and functional level.

This project aims to: (1) compare several approaches to measure the vascularization of the hippocampal formation in vivo and (2) relate the variation in vascularization to the (micro)structural and functional properties across the hippocampal subfields. I am using state-of-the-art UHF MRI techniques, which involves non-invasive measurements of the hippocampal formation's vascularity (vascular density and perfusion), as well as quantification of morphological (volume and thickness) and microstructural (tissue relaxometry, susceptibility and integrity) features. Moreover, I will combine BOLD fMRI with behavioral data to probe differences in functional connectivity across subfields.

— Multi-parametric UHF MRI protocol (in collaboration with dr. Kamil Uludag and dr. Dimo Ivanov)

I will setup an optimized multi-parametric UHF MRI protocol using acquisition schemes that capture fine-scale properties of the hippocampal formation. Here, prime focus will be on the implementation and optimization of existing vascular imaging methods. These will include time-of-flight, susceptibility-weighted and arterial spin labeling imaging. High spatial resolution will be crucial for the proposed experiments, as the structures of interest are small. Image post-processing methods will be optimized to (a) preserve the spatial specificity of the resulting artery, vein and cerebral blood flow (CBF) maps, (b) allow implementation in an in-house developed hippocampal formation coordinate system4 and (c) be combined with other parametric maps.

— Probing subfield integrity

In healthy humans (N=20), I will obtain and analyze the multi-parametric UHF MRI data combined with high resolution resting-state fMRI (rs-fMRI) and behavioral data. Recent work has shown that high resolution rs-fMRI imaging allows for assessing connectivity between hippocampal subfields7. Here, I will validate these findings by disentangling contributions from neuronal activity and other physiological effects such as macroscopic vasculature. In addition, behavioral measures to probe distinct memory subprocesses, such as mismatch detection, pattern separation, and pattern completion, will be employed to correlate with hippocampal subfield integrity based on the UHF MRI data.

The hippocampal formation is of vital importance for proper daily life functioning. It has been linked to episodic memory (i.e., encoding and retrieval of personal past experiences), as well as to other types of declarative memory1, and is therefore one of the most studied structures of the brain. Structural changes of the hippocampal formation, such as reduced volume (i.e. atrophy), are well-established biomarkers in numerous neurodegenerative diseases linked to memory loss.
