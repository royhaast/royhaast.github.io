About

  • 2006 - 2010


    From the beginning of my education and research career, I have been intrigued by neuroscience, and in particular by the combination of Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) and other data modalities. It started during my Biomedical Science bachelor internships at the University of New South Wales, Sydney, Australia (2009) and Radboud University (2009-2010). During these two periods, I developed my interest in neuroscience while performing in vitro molecular and ex vivo histology experiments to study the neural mechanisms underlying brain plasticity.

  • 2010 - 2013


    As I became fascinated by the brain, I enrolled in the neuroscience track of the Medical Biology master at Radboud University. During my first master internship at the Donders Centre for Brain Cognition and Behaviour (2011-2012), I combined vivo MRI and ex vivo histological data to study brain changes in Alzheimerâ€™s disease in a pre-clinical mouse model. In my second internship at the department of Neurology, Radboud University Medical Centre (2012-2013), I applied my acquired knowledge and experience on in vivo human studies.

    Master thesis

  • 2013 - 2018


    Between 2013 and 2018, as PhD student at the department of Cognitive Neuroscience at Maastricht University, I continued this path and combined earlier experiences with novel, state-of-the-art MRI methods to answer clinical-relevant research questions. I developed a multi-parametric ultra-high field (UHF, 7 Tesla) MRI protocol and applied it to study mitochondrial disease (35 participants) and T2DM (almost 150 participants). Specifically, I combined a whole-brain approach with quantitative MRI for (1) parcellating the cortical gray matter, and (2) identification of potential biomarkers in clinical populations.

    Doctoral thesis

  • 2018 - Now


    At the moment I'm working as post-doctoral fellow within the Computational Neuroimaging lab of Dr. Ali Khan at Western University's Centre for Functional and Metabolic Mapping and co-supervised by Dr. Stefan Kohler, head of the Memory Lab in Cognitive Neuroscience at the Brain and Mind Institute. I will continue the line of work that I started during my PhD, but with a strong emphasizes on the hippocampal formation.
